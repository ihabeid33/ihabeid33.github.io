Attack Frameworks and Methodologies

Cyberattacks can manifest in many different forms, from many different threat actors, using many different techniques and forms of malware. One of the many dilemmas an IT security professional will face is how to possibly keep track of and prepare for the many types of threats that emerge every day. Organizations need a way to orient themselves in order to prioritize the most relevant threats, then tailor their defenses and controls to mitigate those threats. Attack frameworks can help organizations and their security analysts find some guidance in identifying possible attacks or security gaps in the network. These frameworks provide a standardize way to describe and present what a possible cyber attack could look like. Some well-known examples of these frameworks include Lockheed Martin's Cyber Kill Chain, the Diamond Model of Intrusion Analysis, or (probably the most widely utilized one) the MITRE ATT&CK framework.

ATT&CK

ATT&CK stands for Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge. It is essentially a well put together knowledge base of tactics, techniques, and procedures (known as 'TTPs' in the industry) that have been observed being used in the wild by threat actors. It was initially started as in internal project by the MITRE corporation to collect data on Advance Persistent Threats, or 'APTs'. APTs are a type of threat actor that specialize in long-term intrusions. They have a large resource pool and funding as they are usually sponsored or run by nation-state governments. Hence, large corporations and U.S. government agencies needed a way to track the TTPs or APTs in order to better protect their networks. Today, ATT&CK is no longer a small internal project, but rather a globally used and open framework.

The Navigator

ATT&CK organizes its knowledge base in different matrices. For example, there is the Enterprise Matrix tailored for standard enterprise organizations, and there's also a ICS Matrix for OT networks containing industrial control systems. These different matrices are presented using a web app called "The Navigator" (shown above). The ATT&CK navigator organizes the knowledge base TTPs with columns of various tactics that contain all of the possible techniques that go with them. For example the "Reconnaissance" column is a tactic that can contain techniques such as "Active Scanning" or "Gather Victim Information".

Tactics

The 'tactics' describe an adversary's objective or goal during a particular stage in an attack. In other words, tactics are the 'why', or the reasoning for an attacker to perform a certain technique. The Navigator presents tactics as the 'columns' in each matrix. Each tactic is also assigned an ID code.

Here is a list of all the possible tactics that can be found in a matrix:

  • Reconnaissance

    • Ways an adversary gathers information on a target.

    • ID: TA0043

  • Resource Development

    • Establishing resources and offensive infrastructure.

    • ID: TA0042

  • Initial Access

    • First foothold into a network.

    • ID: TA0001

  • Execution

    • Adversary running malware.

    • ID: TA0002

  • Persistence

    • Ways an adversary can maintain their foothold.

    • ID: TA0003

  • Privilege Escalation

    • Trying to gain higher-level access or privileges.

    • ID: TA0004

  • Defense Evasion

    • Avoiding Detection and bypassing security controls.

    • ID: TA0005

  • Credential Access

    • Stealing credentials from the organization.

    • ID: TA0006

  • Discovery

    • Sometimes an adversary needs to study the environment they have broken into before taking control of other systems.

    • ID: TA0007

  • Lateral Movement

    • Pivoting through other systems after the initial compromise.

    • ID: TA0008

  • Collection

    • Adversary gathering the data it needs to achieve the attack's initial goal.

    • ID: TA0009

  • Command and Control

    • Ways the attacker can control the systems it compromise using an advanced infrastructure.

    • ID: TA0011

  • Exfiltration

    • Adversary exporting the collected data to finally achieve its initial goal.

    • ID: TA0010

  • Impact

    • Any other goals an adversary wants to accomplish other than data exfiltration. This could be to manipulate data, deface a website, or cause a denial of service.

    • ID: TA0040

Techniques and Procedures

Techniques are the rows placed under one of the fourteen Tactic columns mentioned above. They are generalized methods that describe how an attacker can perform a Tactic. If Tactics are the 'why', then Techniques are the 'how'. For example, an attacker may want to perform the 'Defense Evasion' tactic on a target. To do this, they may choose one of the many techniques that fall under this tactic like 'Masquerading' or 'Hide Artifacts'. A lot of these techniques are also umbrella techniques that contain 'sub-techniques'. For instance, the 'Phishing' technique contains the sub-techniques 'Spearphishing Link' or 'Spearphishing Voice'.

If you click on any technique or sub-technique, it opens a new page that contains information on that technique. There, you will find an overview describing it, and also some example 'Procedures'. Procedures are real-world implementations of the technique that have been observed being performed by APTs. Besides examples of procedures, there may also be some example 'mitigations' and 'detections' that may be useful for defense against that technique.
